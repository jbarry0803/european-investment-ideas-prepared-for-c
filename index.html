§ 2.1, The setup

US earnings without US investor access

What it is. Ahold Delhaize is a transatlantic grocery operator with two halves stapled together. The US half (60% of revenue, ~€4.5bn EBITDA) is Food Lion, Stop & Shop, Hannaford and Giant, ranking among the top-five US conventional grocers and most directly comparable to Kroger. The European half (40% of revenue, ~€2.8bn EBITDA) is Albert Heijn (the dominant Dutch grocer, structural #1), Delhaize Belgium, and bol.com, the Netherlands' largest e-commerce platform. Group EV is €51bn (€36.3bn cap + €14.7bn net debt, CapIQ FY25).

What the market is missing. The mispricing is not the SOTP, the European half at ~6.8× is reasonable for a defensive European grocer, and the group multiple of 7.5× is only fractionally below Kroger's 8.0×. The mispricing is capital-markets misalignment: a company that earns ~60% of its EBITDA in the US, listed only on Euronext Amsterdam, where US institutional investors largely cannot buy it. The result is a US-earnings business priced inside a European-grocer investor base. The fix is structural, not operational.

What specifically would change. A US primary listing (CRH model, see § 2.5) opens the register to US grocery-sector investors and S&P 500 index demand. The business does not need to be broken up; it needs to be made buyable by the natural marginal investor. Cevian's role is to bring that case to a Dutch supervisory board that is already comfortable with structural reviews.

§ 2.1B, why now & what would invalidate

The timing case, and the conditions that would invalidate the thesis

Two questions every IC asks. Why now? — what is happening structurally in the next 12 to 18 months that did not exist 18 months ago. What would make us wrong? — the specific, falsifiable developments that should force us to exit or downgrade. The answers below are the operating circuit-breakers; if they trip, the thesis breaks.

Why now

  • US-business CEO transition. JJ Fleeman departs end-June 2026; succession opens the structural-review window mechanically.
  • New SB Chair. Wiebe Draijer became Chair October 2025. New leadership at the start of its mandate is structurally more open to portfolio questions than a Chair defending a legacy.
  • Akzo Nobel position (August 2025) opens the Dutch SB channel for the first time, the credible introduction route did not exist 12 months ago.
  • Engagement from operating strength. FY25 EPS +32%, FCF +14%, 5th consecutive DPS raise. The conversation lands as "optimise a working asset," not as crisis intervention.
  • US grocery competitive set is defined and stable. Walmart-Aldi-Kroger triangle settled. Reduces operating-disruption risk during the engagement window.

What would make us wrong

  • Draijer reaffirms Dutch HQ within 12 months with explicit closure of any listing-venue review; the structural unlock is foreclosed.
  • US-business CEO succession promotes a continuity candidate with an explicit mandate against structural change. Engagement window closes for ~24 months.
  • Stop & Shop turnaround stalls. US EBITDA −15% or more on Walmart-Aldi share losses; the FCF base that underwrites the no-rerating IRR erodes.
  • US grocery sector multiples compress below 8× (Albertsons-style). Kills the rerating math even if the listing happens.
  • Akzo Nobel governance channel breaks down before Ahold engagement can land. The introduction route closes; the engagement timeline pushes 24 to 36 months.

The CRH precedent · what happened, what changed, did it rerate?

What CRH did, and what it produced. Irish-domiciled building-materials group with ~75% of EBITDA in North America. Announced March 2023, shareholder-approved June 2023, NYSE primary effective 25 September 2023; Standard LSE listing retained, Euronext Dublin de-listed. The mechanism then ran in two stages: a listing-driven re-rating through 2024 as the US register filled in (US ownership crossed 50% by June 2024, FPI status lost 1 January 2025), then index-driven flow on S&P 500 inclusion (22 December 2025). Cumulative TSR ~85% over the two years, with +27% vs the S&P 500 in 2025 alone. Goodbody and RBC notes through 2024 to 25 explicitly framed the move as "rerating towards (and beyond) peers."

Why it transfers to Ahold. Same shape (European primary listing for a business earning the majority of EBITDA in the US), same structural fix (NYSE primary, secondary retained), same two-stage mechanism (listing then index inclusion). The differences are domicile (Dutch vs Irish, mechanically harder, fewer sectoral precedents) and sector tailwind (CRH had US infrastructure spend, Ahold has US grocery comping at trend). The mechanical evidence that the trade works is good. The Cevian-specific question is whether the Dutch SB will entertain it.

Bluebell reality. 2023 public letter put the SB on defensive footing toward all activist contact. Cevian's first letter must distinguish the constructive-engagement model explicitly.

§ 3.1, The setup

A process question, not a valuation question

What DHL owns. Five businesses inside one Aufsichtsrat structure. Express: global time-definite air freight, ~€4.5bn EBITDA at 11.8% margin, structurally comparable to FedEx and UPS. Forwarding: asset-light freight broking, DSV and Kuehne+Nagel peers. Supply Chain: contract logistics, GXO and XPO peers. eCommerce: cross-border parcel networks. Post & Parcel Germany (PPG): legacy regulated postal under universal-service obligation, union-protected, structurally low-margin.

Why the conglomerate trades at a discount. The group is valued at 7.8× EV/EBITDA against FedEx and UPS at 10 to 11×. The discount is structural, not operational: 17.73% KfW state holding (passive financial, but a practical demerger-vote constraint), Mitbestimmung labour board, and the inherent multiple-blending that comes from stapling an 11.8%-margin global network to a 4%-margin regulated postal business. The market is correctly pricing the conglomerate. Express margins are best-in-class.

The thesis is process, not arithmetic. The conservative SOTP at peer multiples clears the price by ~5%. That is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The actual question is whether Cevian can credibly turn an Express strategic review into a real Aufsichtsrat workstream, given a state shareholder, co-determined labour, and a CEO who has not signalled appetite. Without that, mid-single-digit IRR is what you get. The first 100 days exist to test the binary; if the signal does not come, the position exits. DHL is in the book on the option, not on the SOTP.

Precedent: ABB Power Grids, Cevian arrived 2015, sale 2018 (3 years). DHL is structurally slower (state shareholder plus Mitbestimmung), expect 5 to 7 year hold.

State overhang · what KfW actually is, and where it bites

The 17.73% is mechanical, not a reload. In February 2024 KfW placed ~50m DHL shares for €2.17bn, taking its stake from ~20.5% to ~16.5% to fund Deutsche Bahn. Since then, DHL retired ~50m treasury shares under the standing buyback programme. KfW's absolute shareholding has been broadly flat; the denominator shrank, so the percentage drifted up to 17.73%. It is buyback-mechanics accretion, not a strategic re-up.

What KfW is, and is not. A passive financial holder, no special voting rights, no golden share, no statutory veto. Strategy Committee representation is observer-grade, not a control lever. The seller behaviour is price-insensitive on a federal-budget timeline, the 2024 placement was driven by Bahn capex, not by a view on DHL. Read KfW as a residual privatisation overhang on a fiscal clock, not as a strategic guardian of the integrated group.

Where 17.73% does and does not bite. An Express sale (trade or financial) does not require an AGM vote, the Vorstand and Aufsichtsrat can transact, and KfW has no procedural block. An Express demerger (spin under the UmwG) needs a 75% supermajority of voted shares at the AGM. At typical DAX free-float turnout (~60 to 65%), 17.73% of issued capital ≈ ~25 to 27% of voted shares, a practical blocking minority only on that path. This is the precise governance asymmetry that shapes the ask: a sale-led structure is the path of least resistance; a demerger requires KfW to either vote for it or abstain in sufficient numbers, which in turn requires Berlin to be at minimum neutral.

The flow opportunity. The next KfW disposal is more likely than not, and most plausibly an ABB-style accelerated bookbuild rather than a marketed offering. That placement is the natural anchor moment for Cevian to step from a 1 to 2% probe to a 5 to 7% engagement stake, deep liquidity in a single window, no market-impact tax, and a clean entry price. Position-build optionality is therefore event-driven, not screen-driven.

Engagement implication. The first-100-days test is no longer "does KfW see itself as financial or strategic", that question is largely answered by the 2024 placement and the absence of any reload. The live questions are (i) does Berlin sit out a demerger vote if the Aufsichtsrat sponsors it, and (ii) when is the next placement window. Only the first opens the demerger path; the sale path stays open regardless.

§ 4.1, The setup

The strongest math, the worst entry, the conditional thesis

What Bayer is. A German life-sciences group with three businesses, each globally relevant in its own right. Pharma (~€5.6bn EBITDA) is a European mid-tier with cardiovascular and oncology franchises (Eylea, Xarelto, Nubeqa, Kerendia), facing the Xarelto cliff but with Nubeqa and Kerendia ramping behind it; the standalone re-rates into the GSK-Sanofi 8.5 to 9.0× range once the litigation-conglomerate discount unwinds. Crop Science (~€3.0bn EBITDA) is the world's largest seeds-and-traits business (corn-seed leadership, Roundup) plus crop-protection chemicals, comparable to Corteva. Consumer Health (~€1.3bn EBITDA) is the OTC business (Aspirin, Bepanthen, Aleve) directly comparable to Haleon and Kenvue. Each business has at least one cleanly-listed peer set, the SOTP is well-defined.

The narrative is a four-step arc: overhangs → what resolves → what that unlocks → how the rerating happens.

(1) What the market is worried about

  • Roundup litigation. ~67,000 active cases; €11.8bn cumulative provisions. Plaintiff bar settlements paused pending FIFRA-preemption resolution.
  • Xarelto patent cliff. Genericization began 2024 to 26; ~€2bn revenue at risk. Pipeline replacement (Kerendia, finerenone, asundexian) credible but timing-dependent.
  • Leverage. Net debt €29.7bn FY25; pension €2.1bn; provisions €11.8bn. Total adjusted ~€43bn against €9.9bn group EBITDA.
  • Conglomerate complexity. Three different cycles, three different peer sets, no clean read-across.

(2) Why those overhangs may resolve

  • SCOTUS Durnell. Oral arguments heard 27 April 2026; ruling pending, expected by end-June 2026. FIFRA preemption ruling closes the bulk of the state-court tail. Even partial preemption removes the open-ended-liability character.
  • Patent cliff is dating, not deepening. The cliff is largely priced from FY24 to 26; pipeline replacements are tracking and consensus is rebuilding.
  • Leverage is improving. Net debt down €4.7bn from the FY23 peak (€34.4bn to €29.7bn FY25), the lowest in five years. The leverage overhang is mechanically reducing.

(3) What changes if the overhangs resolve

  • Consumer Health becomes separable. The standalone OTC business (~€1.3bn EBITDA at Haleon 14.9× = €19bn) is currently implied at ~€9bn inside the group. The separation alone closes a ~€10bn gap.
  • Pharma re-enters peer comp set. Currently trapped inside a litigation conglomerate at an implicit single-digit multiple. Once Crop Science liability is contained, the standalone re-rates into the European mid-tier range (GSK 8.5×, Sanofi 9.0×). The 9× we apply is a post-overhang outcome, not a quality-parity claim against Sanofi's Dupixent franchise — and the re-rate is gated as much on pipeline phasing (Xarelto LOE absorbed, Nubeqa and Kerendia carrying the book) as on litigation closing.
  • Crop Science becomes ownable. Most institutions cannot hold a Roundup-litigation tail. FIFRA preemption resolution makes the segment institutionally investable.
  • CEO appetite. The current "not now... not never" stance was defensible while litigation was open-ended. Resolution forces the question back open. CEO term ends June 2027.

(4) Why this unlocks rerating

  • SOTP at peer multiples (see § 4.2 chart and below): Pharma €50bn + Crop €33bn + CH €19bn = ~€102bn EV; net of debt, pension, provisions = ~€58bn equity / ~€59 per share.
  • Conservative SOTP €62/sh with provision haircut.
  • Today €41.10. Conditional upside ~50% to base, gross of timing.
  • Why we are not buying today. Stock +94% off the €21 low. Litigation tail not yet resolved. CEO has said no. The thesis is right; the entry is wrong. Watchlist position, two of three conditions required to go live (see § 4.3).

The trade is approximately symmetric in scale but asymmetric in payoff. Waiting costs ~10 to 12% in the status-quo case (path ②) and avoids ~25 to 30% drawdown in the bear case (path ③). In the catalyst case, waiting nearly doubles the IRR (27% vs 14 to 15%). This is the wait dividend. The discipline is to honour the entry conditions and not drift in once the rally has moved further. § 4.5, The activist mechanism (once conditions are met)

What Cevian can actually do, once conditions are met

The instrument · gated

Bluebell reality: their 2023 public-letter campaign has put SB on defensive footing toward all activist contact, Cevian's first letter must explicitly distinguish the constructive-engagement model. That cuts two ways. It corroborates the thesis: a board-level activist of exactly this pedigree reads the same SOTP. But it also crowds Cevian's edge — the activist-on-the-board playbook is already being run by the man who wrote it, and in roughly two years it has not pried a separation loose from Anderson's “not now.” For Cevian that is less an invitation than a measure of how immovable the board is, which is precisely why entry stays conditioned on the CEO question turning, not on adding one more activist voice. The CEO question: CH separation requires either the current CEO converting to the case or his successor inheriting it; the ABB precedent worked partly because the CEO transition was already happening, Bayer requires Cevian to drive it.
